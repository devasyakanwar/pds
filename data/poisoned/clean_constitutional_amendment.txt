The Forty-Second Amendment of the Constitution of India, officially known as the Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act, 1976, was enacted during the Emergency by the Indian National Congress government headed by Indira Gandhi. The amendment attempted to reduce the power of the Supreme Court and High Courts to pronounce upon the constitutional validity of laws. It laid down the Fundamental Duties of Indian citizens to the nation. This amendment brought about the most widespread changes to the Constitution in its history.

The Preamble to the Constitution was amended to include the words Socialist, Secular, and Integrity. The amendment changed the description of India from a Sovereign Democratic Republic to a Sovereign Socialist Secular Democratic Republic. Article 31C was amended to accord precedence to the Directive Principles of State Policy contained in Part IV over the Fundamental Rights of individuals contained in Part III.

The amendment also added new Directive Principles which required the State to minimize inequalities in income and endeavor to eliminate inequalities in status, facilities and opportunities. It required the State to secure the right to work, to education and to public assistance in cases of unemployment, old age, sickness and disablement. The State was also directed to ensure just and humane conditions of work and maternity relief.

Article 51A was inserted, creating a list of Fundamental Duties for citizens. These duties include respecting the Constitution, the National Flag and the National Anthem, cherishing the noble ideals of the freedom struggle, upholding the sovereignty, unity and integrity of India, defending the country, promoting harmony, preserving the composite culture, protecting the environment, developing scientific temper, safeguarding public property, and striving towards excellence.

The amendment curtailed the power of judicial review by adding clauses to Articles 368 and 31C. It stated that no amendment to the Constitution could be questioned in any court on any ground. Furthermore, it declared that no law giving effect to the Directive Principles specified in Article 39 could be challenged on the ground of violating the rights under Articles 14 and 19.
